The Solar Wind
There's no air in space, but it's not a vacuum. The Sun constantly shoots out billions of charged particles (protons and electrons) at insane speeds.

The "Sail" Effect: Even if you don't feel it, these particles hit the surface of your satellite. They create a tiny pressure (solar radiation pressure) that can push the satellite or make it slowly rotate. If you didn't have your copper coils (Magnetorquers) or reaction wheels, after a month the satellite would be pointing toward nothing instead of toward the ground.

Radiation: The solar wind is "radioactive." It can destroy the transistors in normal chips. That's why space computers must be Rad-Hardened (protected from radiation) or have systems that correct errors if a bit goes haywire.

2. Interference
Interference in space isn't like the radio in your car. The challenges will be:

Cosmic Radiation: Particles arriving from other galaxies. They're like tiny bullets passing through the satellite. If they affect your G-ORBIT's memory while it's synchronizing the tables, they can change a "1" to a "0." Your software must be ready to handle these "Single Event Upsets."

Electromagnetic Interference (EMI): Because the satellite is small, the solar panel cables will run close to the antenna. If they're not shielded properly, the "noise" from the current charging the batteries could drown out the weak signals coming from the ground. [[G-ORBIT]]
